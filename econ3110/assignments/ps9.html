Econ 3110: Problem Set 9

Question 1: Cournot Competition (General Derivations)

Setup:

  • Inverse Demand: $P = a - bQ$, where $Q = \sum q_i$.
  • Cost Structure: Constant marginal cost $c_i$ for firm $i$ (no fixed costs).

(a) Duopoly Case ($n=2$)

Firm $i$ seeks to maximize profit $\pi_i = (P - c_i)q_i = (a - b(q_i + q_j) - c_i)q_i$.
The First Order Condition (FOC) with respect to $q_i$ yields the reaction function: $$ \frac{\partial \pi_i}{\partial q_i} = a - bq_j - 2bq_i - c_i = 0 \implies q_i = \frac{a - c_i - bq_j}{2b} $$

Solving the system of reaction functions simultaneously for firms 1 and 2:

  1. $2bq_1 + bq_2 = a - c_1$
  2. $bq_1 + 2bq_2 = a - c_2$

Multiplying (1) by 2 and subtracting (2) allows us to isolate $q_1$: $$ 3bq_1 = a - 2c_1 + c_2 \implies q_1^* = \frac{a - 2c_1 + c_2}{3b} $$ By symmetry, $q_2^* = \frac{a - 2c_2 + c_1}{3b}$.

Equilibrium Price: $$ Q = q_1 + q_2 = \frac{2a - c_1 - c_2}{3b} \implies P^* = a - b\left(\frac{2a - c_1 - c_2}{3b}\right) = \frac{a + c_1 + c_2}{3} $$

(b) Generalization to $n > 2$ Firms

The FOC for any firm $i$ is $a - bQ - bq_i - c_i = 0$ (noting that $\frac{dQ}{dq_i}=1$). Rearranging for $q_i$ gives $q_i = \frac{P - c_i}{b}$.
Summing this expression over all $n$ firms: $$ Q = \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{P - c_i}{b} = \frac{nP - \sum c_i}{b} $$ Substituting the demand curve $P = a - bQ$ into the summation: $$ bQ = n(a - bQ) - \sum c_i \implies bQ(1+n) = na - \sum c_i $$ Thus, total output and price are: $$ Q^* = \frac{na - \sum_{i=1}^n c_i}{b(n+1)} \quad \text{and} \quad P^* = \frac{a + \sum_{i=1}^n c_i}{n+1} $$

(c) Condition for Viability

For a firm to produce a positive quantity ($q_i > 0$), price must exceed its marginal cost ($P^* > c_i$). Substituting our expression for $P^*$: $$ \frac{a + \sum_{j=1}^n c_j}{n+1} > c_i \implies a + \sum_{j \neq i} c_j > n c_i $$ Interpretation: For firm $i$ to remain active in the market, its marginal cost cannot be excessively high relative to the "average" efficiency of the market. Specifically, the sum of the market size parameter $a$ and its rivals' costs must exceed $n$ times its own cost. If a firm is significantly inefficient compared to competitors, it will be priced out of the equilibrium.

(d) Cost Shock Transmission

If every firm's cost increases by $\epsilon$, the new aggregate cost term becomes $\sum c_i + n\epsilon$. $$ P_{new} = \frac{a + \sum c_i + n\epsilon}{n+1} = P_{old} + \frac{n}{n+1}\epsilon $$ Intuition: The equilibrium price rises by less than $\epsilon$ (incomplete pass-through). Because the demand curve is downward-sloping, firms face a trade-off: passing on the full cost would reduce quantity demanded so drastically that profits would plummet. Consequently, the burden of the cost shock is shared; firms absorb a portion of the cost increase ($1 - \frac{n}{n+1}$), while consumers pay the remainder.

Interpretation of Q2(a)

The figure elucidates the distinct strategic incentives between the two market structures. In the Stackelberg framework, the first-mover enjoys a distinct advantage by committing to a high output level (essentially the monopoly quantity adjusted for reaction), forcing subsequent firms to accept residual demand. This results in the first-mover's quantity remaining robust regardless of $n$, while the last-mover's output decays geometrically toward zero.

In contrast, Cournot competition is symmetric; no firm possesses a strategic commitment capability. As $n$ increases, the "external effect" of competition forces every firm to reduce individual output harmoniously, converging toward zero more gradually than the last Stackelberg mover.

Interpretation of Q2(b)

If the objective is to maximize aggregate industry profits, Cournot competition is the superior structure. The intuition is that the sequential nature of Stackelberg competition exacerbates the dissipation of rents. The first-mover's aggressive quantity commitment, combined with the subsequent entry of followers, results in a higher total industry output ($Q$) compared to the symmetric Cournot outcome. This higher aggregate output drives the market price closer to marginal cost, thereby lowering the total surplus available to the producers.

Interpretation of Q2(c)

This figure illustrates a "Pyrrhic victory" for the first-mover. While Firm 1 maintains a significant relative advantage over the later movers (Firm 1 profits > Firm 2 profits), it actually earns less than the symmetric Cournot benchmark. With $n=5$, the aggressive output expansion required to preempt rivals depresses the market price so severely that the volume effect cannot compensate for the price effect. Consequently, all firms, including the leader, would strictly prefer the simultaneous Cournot game to this sequential equilibrium.

Question 3: Collusion and Infinite Horizons

Setup: Symmetric costs $c$, Infinite horizon with discount factor $\delta$.

(a) The Collusive Benchmark ($\pi_C$)

Under perfect collusion, firms act as a single monopolist maximizing $\Pi = (a - bQ - c)Q$.
The monopoly quantity is $Q^* = \frac{a-c}{2b}$.
Total monopoly profit is $\frac{(a-c)^2}{4b}$.
Since this is shared equally among $n$ firms: $$ \pi_C = \frac{(a-c)^2}{4nb} $$

(b) The Temptation to Defect ($\pi_D$)

If rivals produce their share of the cartel output, $Q_{-i} = (n-1)\frac{a-c}{2nb}$.
Firm $i$ maximizes against this fixed output. The residual demand intercept is $A = a - b Q_{-i} = a - \frac{n-1}{2n}(a-c)$. Firm $i$'s best response is $\tilde{q}_i = \frac{A-c}{2b}$. Algebraically simplifying yields: $$ \tilde{q}_i = \frac{a-c}{2b} \left( \frac{n+1}{2n} \right) $$ The resulting deviation profit is: $$ \pi_D = \frac{(a-c)^2}{4b} \left( \frac{n+1}{2n} \right)^2 $$

(c) The Punishment: Cournot ($\pi_G$)

From our earlier work in Q1(b), the symmetric Cournot profit is: $$ \pi_G = \frac{(a-c)^2}{b(n+1)^2} $$

(d) Credibility of Grim Trigger

Cournot competition serves as a suitable punishment because it constitutes a Nash Equilibrium of the stage game. Unlike a threat to set price to zero (which would hurt the punisher), reverting to Cournot is incentive-compatible; once the punishment phase begins, no firm has an incentive to deviate from the punishment strategy. This makes the threat Subgame Perfect.

(e) Valuation of Strategies

  • Value of Sustained Collusion: Sum of a geometric series. $$ V_C = \sum_{t=0}^{\infty} \delta^t \pi_C = \frac{\pi_C}{1-\delta} $$
  • Value of Deviation: One period of high profit $\pi_D$, followed by $\pi_G$ forever. $$ V_D = \pi_D + \frac{\delta \pi_G}{1-\delta} $$

(f) The Critical Discount Factor

Collusion is sustainable if $V_C \ge V_D$. Rearranging the inequality: $$ \frac{\pi_C}{1-\delta} \ge \pi_D + \frac{\delta \pi_G}{1-\delta} \implies \delta (\pi_D - \pi_G) \ge \pi_D - \pi_C $$ $$ \delta \ge \frac{\pi_D - \pi_C}{\pi_D - \pi_G} $$ Intuition: The numerator represents the immediate "temptation" (short-term gain), while the denominator represents the "punishment severity" (long-term loss). Firms will only collude if they are sufficiently patient (high $\delta$) to prioritize the stream of future cooperative profits over the one-time windfall from cheating.

(g) Partial Collusion

Answer: Yes, the threshold $\delta$ could be lower. Intuition: If firms agree to collude at a quantity $\hat{q}$ that is closer to the Cournot level (less restrictive than monopoly output), the profit $\pi_C$ is lower, but the incentive to deviate decreases drastically. Because the market price is lower than the monopoly price, the marginal gain from undercutting is smaller. If the reduction in temptation outweighs the reduction in cooperative profit, the cartel becomes easier to sustain, requiring less patience (a lower $\delta$). form the members.
